Bible Seminar 1 - The church is God's new creation (Acts 2:1-41)

Theme: As Jesus promised, the gathered people of God receive the Holy Spirit and begin to call people from every nation to receive forgiveness and the Spirit as they have. The new covenant creation begins here and starts on its mission to fill the whole earth!
Aim: See the privilege of being gathered to God's Spirit-filled new covenant community. Listen and have confidence that forgiveness and the gift of the Spirit is available for you and your friends through repentance and faith in Jesus.

Text notes

The new covenant begins with the gift of the Spirit for all (2:1-21)

The disciples have obeyed Jesus' command to gather in Jerusalem and wait for the gift of the Spirit that will enable them to fulfil the mission he has given them (1:8). Although there are around 120 gathered (1:15), Luke has drawn our attention to the fact that the number of apostles is back up to 12 (1:26), and that it's now Pentecost. This festival is also called the Feast of Weeks in the OT -- it's a harvest festival, 50 days after Passover, and so it has a sense of completion, joy, and fulfilment -- and that's what we get here. In Jewish thought it had also begun to be associated with the giving of the Law at Mount Sinai (which happened 2 months after the first Passover, Exod 19:1, so roughly the same time difference). And if you know what happened at Mount Sinai, it all sounds very familiar! The anointed leader goes up to God (Moses/Jesus), the divine presence (a great noise and fire, Exod 19:16-19) comes down on 12 tribes/people, there is a gift of God which marks a new beginning for his people (the Law/the Spirit), and a sermon is preached which calls for obedience. After the Passover and the Exodus came the old covenant; after Jesus' cross and resurrection comes the new covenant!
But there are both similarities and differences between the two experiences. The crowd outside is not a united people, but a scattered people who are re-gathered from every nation. This is the fulfilment of prophecies such as Isaiah 66:18-23 and Jeremiah 31:10-34 - the ingathering of the exiled people of Israel to Jerusalem to be his people once again. But the people's reaction to the new covenant is perhaps not ideal. Some are interested and amazed, but others are mocking (2:12-13) -- just like the crowds were when Jesus spoke to them, and a foretaste of the response the church will get when they preach the gospel from now on.
But there's a good difference too! Previously, Moses was the only Spirit-filled mediator between God and man, and he wished that everyone in Israel could enjoy God's presence in the same way he did (Numbers 11:27-29). Now everyone does! God's fiery presence by his Spirit rests on "all of them" (2:4) without destroying them -- God has come to live with his people once and for all! And as Peter explains with his quote from Joel 2, this is what God always intended (2:14-21).
OT Context
Joel 2 is worth looking at closely - in it Joel:
  • prophesies the judgement of the Lord on his sinful people, via the nations coming like locusts to take the people into exile (Joel 2:1-11);
  • calls for the right response - that the people should gather together and repent (Joel 2:12-17);
  • says that the judgement will be turned away and a permanent blessing poured out, but only after a great cataclysm which will be like the end of the world (Joel 2:18-27 -- see Luke 23:44-45);
  • then prophesies that the Spirit will be poured out on every member of God's people -- not just the prophets, priests, and kings but men and women, boys and girls, masters and servants -- and that when that happens salvation will be available to everyone (Joel 2:14-21).

The new covenant begins with the offering of the Son for all (2:22-36)

After explaining what's going on, Peter takes the gathered crowd back to the event which has made this new covenant possible -- the death, resurrection, and ascension of Jesus Christ, the new and final Passover sacrifice.
The work of the Spirit
It's notable that when the Spirit comes on the people, the first thing that happens is that they all begin to speak God's word and that the theme of their speech is the gospel of Jesus. This is what the Spirit always does -- he shines a spotlight on Jesus with clear, intelligible words, and helps people to respond with repentance and faith (see John 16:7-11, Eph 6:17, 1 Cor 14:19). And the "tongues" here are clearly normal human languages -- Pentecost seems to reverse the curse of Babel by allowing people from every nation to once again gather to understand the word of God!
So don't get bogged down in this session with the apparent "weirdness" of the Spirit's manifestation -- as we can see from the OT context, the Spirit comes to reveal God's plans and make things clearer, not make things more complicated! This is also clearly a spectacular, one-off Sinai-ish experience, not something we should expect to see repeated. There are a few "mini-Pentecosts" in the book of Acts for different groups of people corresponding to the programme of Acts 1:8 (Samaritans in Acts 8, Gentiles in Acts 10, the remainder of the faithful remnant of Israel in Acts 19), but most people are converted and receive the Spirit without anything visibly or audibly spectacular! The 14-18s will get a chance to explore this more thoroughly in the following session.
Peter proves this to the people with the help of three witness:
  1. Their own experience (2:22-24). This is the same crowd who have seen and heard Jesus' "miracles, wonders and signs" (note the Exodus language, Exodus 7:3) which were God's proof of who he was. But instead of accepting him, they colluded with the Gentiles to put him to death -- yet this was all part of God's plan for Jesus to be the true Passover Lamb (2:23). Given Jesus' status as God's chosen king, and sinless perfection which meant he did not deserve to die, it was "impossible for death to keep its hold on him" -- and the people who've been in Jerusalem for the whole time know all this.
  2. David's prophecy (2:25-32). Peter recalls Psalm 16, where King David commits himself to follow God no matter what, and is confident that he will receive an unshakeable and eternal inheritance - his body will never see decay. But Peter rather ironically points his finger to David's grave (2:29)! So how can David say this? It's because David is a prophet (2:30) -- the true singer of this Psalm is David's greater Son Jesus, and Jesus' resurrection is the proof that Jesus is the true Messiah of Israel.
  3. God's vindication (2:33-36). From Jesus' death and resurrection, Peter moves on to Jesus' ascension, which the apostles witnessed less than a fortnight ago (2:32-33, see 1:9). Another prophetic quote from King David explains the point - having been apparently crushed by his enemies, Jesus is now risen and ruling over every nation and will do so until every enemy is destroyed (2:34-35; see Psalm 110:1, which Jesus himself drew attention to in Luke 20:41-44).

The new covenant begins with the grace of the Father to all (2:37-31)

The people respond as Joel says they should -- with the conviction that only the Spirit can bring (2:37, c.f. 1 Thess 1:5). They know they are sinners who have mistreated Jesus and they now fear God's righteous judgement. But wonderfully, there is mercy available for them -- they too can repent, trust in Jesus, be baptised, and receive the same Holy Spirit (2:38)! But it's not just for them - as the OT prophesied, it's for both Jews and "far off" Gentiles (2:39), and so the message must go out to "the ends of the earth" (1:8).
One final fulfilment of Sinai remains. When Moses came down from the mountain, he found the people worshipping a golden calf -- and the Law which he held in his hands, written on stone, couldn't save or change them (c.f. 2 Cor 3:5-9). In judgement God put to death 3000 people (Exod 32:28). But now Jesus has poured out his Spirit, bringing conviction of sin and turning away from idols -- and his death, resurrection and ascension makes it possible for all to be forgiven and the exiles gathered in. And the result? 3000 people are brought to life (2:41).
